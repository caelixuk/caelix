# Exp06 — Relativity proxy (Oscillators + Lensing + Carrier diagnostics)

## Abstract

Exp06 demonstrates that the emergent scalar potential field (\(\phi\)) acts as a **variable refractive index** (\(n>1\)), deriving weak-field General Relativistic phenomena from local lattice transport.

By coupling the telegraph (wave) solver to a static potential background generated via relaxation, we verify two fundamental predictions of curved spacetime:

1) **Gravitational lensing:** rays bend **towards** the mass, following the weak-field deflection law \(\theta\propto 1/b\).
2) **Gravitational time dilation (phase drift):** driven oscillators deeper in the potential well tick **slower** than those in the far field (\(T_{\text{near}} > T_{\text{far}}\)), confirming that potential depth produces time retardation.

Exp06 also includes a **carrier qualification** sub-test (06B) which does not claim relativity directly. It validates that the telegraph medium supports stable propagation and late-time ringdown modes under the same kernel parameters used by 06A.

This experiment is therefore a confirmation, not a feasibility test: a static potential influences wave propagation and local clock-rate proxies in a repeatable, quantitative way.

## Theoretical basis: gravity as refraction

In Exp06, gravity is not implemented as geometric curvature of the grid (which remains Cartesian). Instead, it appears as a modulation of local wave speed \(c(\mathbf{x})\) — the optical analogue of gravity.

We construct an effective refractive index field \(n(\mathbf{x})\) by coupling to the scalar potential background \(\phi_{\mathrm{bg}}\):

\[
 n(\mathbf{x}) = 1 + \alpha\,\phi_{\mathrm{bg}}(\mathbf{x})
\]

where \(\alpha>0\) is a coupling constant.

- **High potential (near mass):** \(n>1\Rightarrow v_{\text{light}}<c\). Waves slow down.
- **Low potential (far field):** \(n\to 1\Rightarrow v_{\text{light}}\to c\).

Predictions:

- **Lensing:** a wavefront passing the mass experiences a transverse speed gradient (slower on the near side), curving the ray inward. The mass behaves as a **converging lens**.
- **Dilation:** a driven standing-wave cavity in the high-\(n\) region has a reduced resonant frequency (heuristically \(\omega\propto c/n\)), producing a measurable phase lag relative to the far-field probe.

## Theoretical expectations

### Lensing in a weak refractive medium

If wave propagation can be described by a refractive index field \(n(\\mathbf{x})\\) (slowly varying compared to the wavelength), geometric optics predicts curvature toward higher index:

\\[
\\frac{d\\boldsymbol{\\theta}}{ds}\\;\\propto\\;\\nabla_{\\perp}\\,\\ln n
\\]

For a spherically symmetric weak field where \\ln n\\sim 1/r\\, the integrated deflection for a straight-line pass at impact parameter \(b\) scales as:

\\[
\\theta(b)\\;\\propto\\;\\frac{1}{b}
\\]

This \(1/b\) law is the primary lensing signature we look for.

### Time dilation as phase drift

Two identical oscillators driven at the same angular frequency \(\\omega\) and sampled over many cycles allow a clean measurement of relative phase drift:

\\[
\\Delta\\phi(t) = \\phi_{\\text{near}}(t) - \\phi_{\\text{far}}(t),\\qquad \\frac{d}{dt}\\Delta\\phi \\neq 0
\\]

A constant drift rate implies a small frequency difference:

\\[
\\Delta f \\approx \\frac{1}{2\\pi}\\,\\frac{d}{dt}\\Delta\\phi
\\]

The sign of the effect depends on how the potential couples into wave speed / index (see “Coupling conventions” below).

## Methodology and constraints

What the evolution **does**:

- Builds a static background potential \\phi_{\\mathrm{bg}}\\ by iterative relaxation (diffusion-style transport), identical in spirit to Exp01.
- Runs telegraph-mode evolution for oscillators and rays in the presence of that static background.
- Measures lensing deflection and oscillator phase drift using transparent, auditable diagnostics.

What the evolution **does not** do:

- It does **not** hard-code curvature, geodesics, or GR field equations.
- It does **not** use complex quantum phases or external “clock” code.
- It does **not** fit results to GR constants; we only check scaling/consistency.

A key “anti-fudge” point: the background field is not a handcrafted \(1/r\) function. It is generated by the same local transport dynamics that produced the earlier emergent potential experiments.

## Experimental results (06A, fixed sign)

This section records the measured outcomes from the successful **06A** run after correcting the ray-bending sign convention.

### Gravitational lensing (light bending)

Analysis of ray trajectories confirms the weak-field deflection law. For rays with impact parameters \(b>20\), the deflection angle \(\theta\) follows the predicted scaling:

\[
\theta(b) \approx \frac{0.068}{b}
\]

- Relationship: confirmed \(\theta \propto b^{-1}\) (inverse impact parameter).
- Lensing strength \(A\): **0.0681** (the effective \(4GM/c^2\) of the source in this model).
- Deflection direction: **converging** (inward). Rays launched at \(y>0\) deflect by \(\sim -1.0\) mrad (downwards toward the mass).
- Fit quality: high adherence to \(1/b\) for \(b>20\).

### Gravitational time dilation (phase drift)

Comparison of identical driven oscillators at \(r_{\text{near}}=16\) and \(r_{\text{far}}=160\) reveals a measurable desynchronisation.

- Far clock frequency \(\omega_{\text{far}}\): **0.149997** rad/tick
- Near clock frequency \(\omega_{\text{near}}\): **0.149996** rad/tick
- Frequency shift \(\Delta\omega\): **\(-1.0\times 10^{-6}\)** rad/tick
- Time dilation factor \(T_{\text{near}}/T_{\text{far}}\): **1.000006**

The near oscillator runs slower by approximately **6 parts per million**, verifying that potential depth \(\phi\) retards the local rate of time evolution (\(T_{\text{near}}>T_{\text{far}}\)).

## Coupling conventions

Exp06 turns the static potential into an optical index field:

\\[
 n(\\mathbf{x}) = 1 + \\alpha\\,\\phi_{\\mathrm{bg}}(\\mathbf{x})
\\]

- \\alpha>0\\ means higher potential increases index.
- Rays should bend **toward** higher index.

Note: a sign error in early ray-marching code can invert the lens (producing a diverging lens). The correct convention is that the ray angle update must follow the gradient toward higher \(n\\).

## Sub-test A: Oscillator phase drift

### Geometry

Two identical driven probes are placed on a radius line from the central mass:

- near radius: `osc-r-near`
- far radius: `osc-r-far`

Both probes are driven in-phase with a sinusoidal source at angular frequency `osc-omega` and amplitude `osc-amp`.

### Warm-up vs burn

Two time windows matter:

- `osc-warm`: **pre-run** ticks before recording begins. Warm-up advances the drive phase and allows the near probe (in the slow-light region) to settle into a steady standing-wave regime.
- `osc-burn`: initial recorded window excluded from fitting.

Experimental protocol:

- We evolve for \(T_{\text{warm}}\) ticks before recording the phase series. This removes the initial ring-up / “shock” distortion that can appear when the potential background is suddenly present.
- Burn is then applied as a fitting gate to ensure only steady behaviour contributes to the phase-drift estimate.

### Measurement

The per-sample traces are recorded at cadence `osc-sample-every`. Phase drift is estimated using a complex demodulation at the drive frequency and a weighted linear slope fit of the unwrapped phase difference.

The summary CSV reports:

- near/far mean background potentials
- measured near/far frequencies and \(\\Delta f\\)
- drift rate \(d\\Delta\\phi/dt\)
- provenance parameters for reproducibility

## Sub-test B: Lensing preview

### Rays

Rays are launched across the field with varying impact parameter \(b\\) and marched with step size `osc-ray-step` for up to `osc-ray-max` steps.

A minimal geometric-optics marching scheme is used:

- local direction is updated from the transverse gradient of \\(\\ln n\\)
- position is advanced along the updated direction

### Primary check

From the final ray headings we measure a deflection angle \\(\\theta\\) for each ray and check whether:

- \\(\\theta\\) scales as \\(\\approx A/b\\)
- the intercept is near zero
- the fit is stable when excluding very small \\(|b|\\) passes (rays effectively “through” the source)

The exact coefficient \(A\\) is not treated as fundamental here; the scaling law is the target.

## How to read the result

Suggested checks:

1) **Background stability:** ensure the background build uses sufficient iterations for the chosen \(n\). If under-converged, both phase and ray results can be biased by transient drift.
2) **Oscillator trace quality:** confirm `osc-warm` removes early distortion and that the post-burn window shows stable sinusoidal behaviour.
3) **Phase drift sign:**
   - Correct gravity: the near phase should **lag** the far phase (\(\\Delta\\phi<0\) after unwrapping), implying \(\\omega_{\\text{near}}<\\omega_{\\text{far}}\) (redshift).
   - Anti-gravity signature: if near **leads** far, the effective coupling has produced \(n<1\) (diverging / repulsive behaviour).
4) **Lensing scaling:** fit \(\\theta\\) vs \(b\) on a log-log plot.
   - Success: the data should form a straight line with slope \(-1\) (\(\\theta\\propto b^{-1}\)), consistent with the Schwarzschild weak-field limit.
   - Direction: deflection must correspond to **inward** curvature (converging lens) under the chosen sign convention.
5) **Exclude pathological passes:** rays with very small \(|b|\) should be treated cautiously; they probe the discrete “core” where the weak-field approximation is not expected to hold.

### 06D result: threshold + flight-gate phase-lock in the lightbox

06D records a **reproducible baseline artefact** in the lightbox when using a *thresholded first-crossing* arrival proxy combined with a *flight-time gate* and immediate pulse reinjection. In this regime the apparatus behaves as a **gated relaxation oscillator**: the measured period is dominated by algorithmic phase-locking rather than transport kinematics.

In a smoke test on 2026-02-25 (run IDs `ZZ6_relativity_twin_paradox_n512_20260225_103804.csv` and `ZZ6_relativity_twin_paradox_n512_20260225_110513.csv`), using telegraph transport with:

- `n=512`, `traffic_mode=telegraph`, `c2=0.31`, `dt=1.0`, `gamma=0.001`, `decay=0.0`
- mirror separation `L=48`, slab thickness `slab=8`
- two drifting clocks (fast/slow) to avoid axis-aligned shots
- Schmitt-style patch-energy detector (5×5×slab) with refractory gate

we observed the following baseline behaviour:

1) With a higher threshold (`rel-threshold=0.05`), hits can occur on tails or late shoulders and the cadence becomes jittery.
2) With a lower threshold (`rel-threshold=0.01`), both clocks survive reliably but the detector **phase-locks** to the gate: after the initial leg, every leg is accepted at a fixed offset after the gate opens.

Crucially, the detector is not timing the packet crest: at the accepted hit tick, patch energy can be orders of magnitude below the subsequent local maximum. The true peak times in the same windows retain a small but consistent fast-vs-slow delay, yet the first-crossing rule can erase this by triggering on different parts of the envelope (a shape/attenuation artefact).

**Implication:** a fixed absolute threshold + first-crossing logic is not a valid “arrival time” proxy for the lightbox, and can mask (or invert) the intended kinematic effect by snapping the apparatus onto a gate-locked attractor.

**Suggested fix (for a future 06D+ re-validation):** replace first-crossing with *windowed peak detection*: once the flight gate opens and energy crosses a low noise-floor threshold, buffer a short window (e.g. 10–20 ticks), register the local maximum as the arrival time, and reinject based on that peak time. This breaks the digital phase lock and decouples timing from envelope slope.

### 06E result: windowed peak arrival timing breaks gate-lock (run 06E)

06E implements the suggested fix from 06D by switching the arrival proxy from **first-crossing threshold** to **windowed peak detection** (12‑tick window after crossing a low noise-floor). This removes the digital phase-lock attractor and allows the clocks' *crest times* to express the intended kinematic separation.

**Run artefact:** `06E_relativity_twin_paradox_window_peak_n512_20260225_130706.csv`

**Configuration:** telegraph transport, `n=512`, `c2=0.31`, `dt=1.0`, `gamma=0.001`, `decay=0.0`, mirror separation `L=48`, slab thickness `slab=8`, fast/slow drifting clocks (`v_frac=0.3`, `v_ref_frac=0.1`), detector `rel-detect=window_peak`, `rel-start-threshold=0.01`, `rel-peak-window=12`.

#### Outcome

1) **Gate-lock is broken.** Per-leg crest timings are no longer quantised to a single attractor (contrast 06D's 92‑tick legs). Leg times fluctuate with the packet envelope and local lattice state.

2) **Kinematic separation appears in the crest-timed A→A periods.** Using the peak-time stamps (`hit_t_*`) for the first five completed round trips:

- Slow clock A peaks: 194, 398, 578, 774, 957, 1153 → periods: 204, 180, 196, 183, 196 (mean **191.8** ticks)
- Fast clock A peaks: 194, 405, 602, 791, 982, 1171 → periods: 211, 197, 189, 191, 189 (mean **195.4** ticks)

This yields a measured ratio `T0/Tp ≈ 191.8 / 195.4 ≈ 0.982`, i.e. the fast clock ticks slower (larger period), consistent with the intended diagonal path-length stretching.

3) **Late-run failure mode: echo-triggered early capture window.** By the final cycle the slab contains substantial wake/echo energy. The slow clock's capture window was triggered by a background shoulder and accepted a small local peak, then the true crest arrived a few ticks later after the detector had already committed:

- Slow clock accepted A at `t=1361` with `e_sa≈2.46×10^5` (peak logged at the same tick).
- The true crest followed shortly after (`t=1367` in this run) with `e_sa≈1.00×10^8`, but was ignored because the expected-mirror state had already advanced.

As a consequence, the last reported single-cycle estimates at the end of the run use a corrupted slow period (`T0_est=208`) against a typical fast period (`Tp_est=190`), producing `ratio_est≈0.913`. This should not be interpreted as the kinematic ratio; it is an “echo pollution” artefact of bounded domains.


### 06F result: lateral XY sponge suppresses echo pollution (run 06F)

06F keeps the 06E windowed peak detector but adds an absorbing boundary layer on the **outer X/Y faces** to prevent late-time boundary returns from raising the interior noise floor.

A first attempt with `sponge_width=32` failed (no hits) because the lightbox starts near `x≈14` to provide runway, which lies inside a 32-cell X-face sponge. Narrowing the sponge to 8 cells resolves this while still damping lateral returns.

**Run artefact:** `06F_relativity_twin_paradox_xy_sponge_n512_20260225_144735.csv`

**Configuration:** telegraph transport, `n=512`, `c2=0.31`, `dt=1.0`, `gamma=0.001`, `decay=0.0`, mirror separation `L=48`, slab thickness `slab=8`, fast/slow drifting clocks (`v_frac=0.3`, `v_ref_frac=0.1`), detector `rel-detect=window_peak`, `rel-start-threshold=0.01`, `rel-peak-window=12`, plus `boundary_mode=sponge`, `sponge_axes=xy`, `sponge_width=8`, `sponge_strength=1.0`.

#### Outcome

1) **Echo ambush removed.** The run completes with sustained ticking and no late-cycle “false window” capture. Total accepted hits: `hits_sb=10`, `hits_mb=10`, with `hits_ma=10` and `hits_sa=9` over 2000 steps.

2) **Stable timing estimates at end of run.** The estimator settles to:

- `T0_est=201`, `Tp_est=191`, `ratio_est=0.950` (as reported in the CSV)

On this lattice, diagonal propagation is measurably faster than axis-aligned propagation (`c_diag_est≈0.530` vs `c_axis_est≈0.478`, giving `anisotropy_A≈0.901`). This anisotropy can dominate the geometric path-length effect, so the *raw* ratio can be <1 even when the geometric path is longer.

3) **Anisotropy-corrected kinematic factor is consistent.** Using the CSV's correction pipeline, the inferred kinematic factor is stable and close to the special-relativistic value for `v_frac=0.3`:

- `gamma_corr_est≈1.054` vs `gamma_sr≈1.048`

**Interpretation:** 06F demonstrates that a thin lateral sponge layer is sufficient to suppress echo pollution and stabilise long runs. It also makes explicit that any “moving lightbox” proxy must account for lattice anisotropy (axis vs diagonal effective speed) when interpreting raw tick ratios.
